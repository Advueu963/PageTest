Probability Estimation via Calibration#

The predictions delivered by corresponding methods are at best “pseudo-probabilities” that are often not very accurate. Besides, there are many methods that deliver natural scores, intuitively expressing a degree of confidence (like the distance from the separating hyperplane in support vector machines), but which do not immediately qualify as probabilities either. The idea of scaling or calibration methods is to turn such scores into proper, well-calibrated probabilities, that is, to learn a mapping from scores to the unit interval that can be applied to the output of a predictor as a kind of post-processing step (Flach [Fla17]). Examples of such methods include binning (Zadrozny and Elkan [ZE01]), isotonic regression (Zadrozny and Elkan [ZE02]), logistic scaling (Platt [Pla99]) and improvements thereof (Kull et al. [KdMFF17]), as well as the use of Venn predictors (Johansson et al. [JLofstromS+18]). Calibration is still a topic of ongoing research.
